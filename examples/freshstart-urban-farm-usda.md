## OUTPUT 2: Executive Summary & Needs Statement

### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

In North Las Vegas, a family without a car lives 3.2 miles from the nearest grocery store — and the bus takes 45 minutes each way. For the 34% of Clark County households that are food insecure, access is not a choice problem; it's a geography problem that fresh produce makes worse every year.

FreshStart Urban Farm Collective has operated two urban farms in food desert zip codes since 2019, producing 4,800 pounds of fresh vegetables annually for 340 low-income households. This USDA Community Food Projects grant will fund our next phase: a 12-month expansion adding a third farm site, a youth job training program placing 20 young adults in paid agricultural employment, and a sliding-scale CSA delivering weekly produce boxes to 150 additional families at or below 185% of the federal poverty level.

Expected outcomes by grant year end:
- 150 additional households receiving consistent, affordable fresh produce weekly (500+ total)
- 20 youth ages 14-24 completing 200-hour paid agricultural workforce training
- 6,000+ additional pounds of fresh produce grown and distributed in Clark County
- 3 permanent part-time positions created at living wage ($18/hr)

$75,000 from the Community Food Projects program will unlock $42,000 in matching commitments from the Nevada Community Foundation and in-kind land use from Clark County Parks — making this grant the catalyst that doubles FreshStart's community reach in one year.

---

### NEEDS STATEMENT

Food insecurity affects 34 million Americans, with rates rising in Western states hit hardest by post-pandemic housing cost increases (USDA Economic Research Service, 2024). Nevada ranks 11th nationally in food insecurity, with 13.1% of households — approximately 160,000 people — lacking consistent access to adequate food (Feeding America, Map the Meal Gap, 2023).

In Clark County, the crisis is concentrated. North Las Vegas and Sunrise Manor — the two zip codes where FreshStart operates — report food insecurity rates of 22% and 19% respectively, nearly double the national average (Nevada Food Bank, 2023 Community Needs Assessment). Both neighborhoods are USDA-designated food deserts: areas where 33% or more of the population lives more than 1 mile from a supermarket without access to a vehicle.

The families most affected are those least equipped to absorb the cost. A single mother working two minimum-wage jobs in North Las Vegas earns approximately $2,400/month after taxes. When the nearest grocery store is a 45-minute bus ride, she pays in time she doesn't have — or substitutes with corner store food that costs more and delivers less nutrition. Her children's diets reflect this: Clark County pediatric obesity rates in food desert zip codes run 28% versus 17% countywide (SNHD Health Data Report, 2022).

Existing solutions fail because they treat symptoms, not geography. Food pantries provide emergency relief but require transportation and have limited produce. SNAP benefits provide purchasing power but not access — there's nothing to buy if the store is inaccessible. Clark County's one existing community garden has a 3-year waitlist. No other urban farm operates in the target zip codes.

The urgency is now. Clark County's population grew by 42,000 in 2023 (US Census Bureau), adding demand to an already undersupplied food system. Meanwhile, three urban lots adjacent to FreshStart's existing farm site will become available for lease in January 2026 — a one-time opportunity to triple growing capacity before they're converted to commercial use.

FreshStart's urban farm model eliminates the access barrier by bringing production inside the food desert. The 2026 expansion — three farm sites, youth employment, and a sliding-scale CSA — will serve 500+ households with fresh produce grown within two miles of their homes.

## OUTPUT 3: Project Narrative & Work Plan

### PROJECT NARRATIVE

**Program Description**

The FreshStart 2026 Expansion Program will operate three urban farm sites totaling 1.2 acres in North Las Vegas's 89030 and 89115 zip codes, produce a minimum of 10,000 pounds of fresh vegetables annually, and distribute that produce to 500+ low-income households through a sliding-scale Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) model and direct on-site distribution. Simultaneously, the program will employ 20 young adults ages 14-24 in a 200-hour paid agricultural workforce training cohort.

A typical participant's journey: A family contacts FreshStart through their child's school (one of 6 partner schools) or a referral from Three Square Food Bank. They complete a 10-minute intake form and are enrolled in the CSA at the rate appropriate for their income ($0-$15/week on a sliding scale based on household size and income). Each Thursday, they pick up their box at the farm site — which also serves as a community gathering point. After 3 months, participants receive a cooking skills workshop and nutritional assessment. Year-end outcome data measures food security improvement, produce consumption, and household budget impact.

**Evidence Base**

Urban farm CSA models have demonstrated measurable food security outcomes in comparable food desert communities. A study of 12 urban agriculture projects in the Southwest found a 34% reduction in food insecurity scores among regular CSA participants versus a control group after 6 months (Twiss et al., American Journal of Public Health, 2003). The USDA's Community Food Projects program itself has documented that community-controlled food production systems outperform distribution-only models on long-term household food security by 2:1 (USDA NIFA 2022 Grantee Outcomes Report). FreshStart's own data from 2021-2023 shows that families receiving weekly CSA boxes for 12+ months reported a 41% reduction in emergency food pantry visits.

The youth workforce component draws from evidence in urban agriculture workforce programs: a National 4-H Council evaluation of 8 urban farm training programs found 78% of participants secured employment or continued education within 6 months of completion, with starting wages averaging $2.40/hr above minimum wage.

**Organizational Capacity**

FreshStart has operated urban farms in Clark County since 2019. Our team includes a Certified Master Gardener (Executive Director, 12 years urban agriculture experience), a licensed social worker managing intake and wraparound services, and 4 part-time farm staff. We hold a formal MOU with Three Square Food Bank (Nevada's largest food bank) for referrals and surplus distribution, and a lease agreement with Clark County Parks for our existing two farm sites. Our 2023-2024 season produced 4,800 pounds of vegetables serving 340 households — a production efficiency of 14 pounds/square foot that exceeds the national urban farm average of 9 pounds/square foot.

**Sustainability**

Post-grant sustainability is built into the program design. The CSA subscription model generates earned revenue: at full capacity (500 households × $8/week average × 50 weeks), the CSA generates $200,000 annually — more than covering operating costs without grant dependence. Year 1 of this grant establishes that subscriber base. Additionally, FreshStart has a $30,000 multi-year commitment from the Nevada Community Foundation beginning Year 2, a pending proposal to the W.K. Kellogg Foundation for $150,000 over 3 years, and a board-approved earned income strategy targeting $80,000 in CSA revenue by grant year end.

**Risk Mitigation**

Risk 1 — Extreme heat disrupting growing season: Las Vegas summers regularly exceed 115°F, limiting outdoor production June-August. Mitigation: shade structures on new farm site (budgeted in capital line), pivot to heat-tolerant crops (Armenian cucumber, Malabar spinach, sweet potato), expand fall/winter production season to compensate.

Risk 2 — Youth recruitment shortfall for training cohort: Target population (14-24, food desert zip codes) has transportation barriers and competing time demands. Mitigation: Partnership MOUs with 6 CCSD schools and two workforce development agencies (Nevada JobConnect and Goodwill of Southern Nevada) provide referral pipeline. Stipend structure ($12/hr for training hours) removes financial disincentive.

## OUTPUT 4: Evaluation Framework & Budget Justification

### EVALUATION PLAN

**Evaluation Questions:**
1. Did the CSA program increase household food security among enrolled families, and by how much?
2. Did youth participants complete training, and did they secure employment or continue education within 6 months?
3. Did the program produce the targeted volume of fresh produce at the target cost-per-pound efficiency?
4. Did community partnerships deliver the referral volume needed to sustain enrollment targets?

**Indicators & Data Collection:**

| Objective | Indicator | Data Source | Frequency | Baseline | Target |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|----------|--------|
| 500 households receive CSA weekly | # active CSA subscribers | CSA enrollment database | Monthly | 340 households (2024) | 500 by Month 6 |
| 20 youth complete 200-hr training | # youth completing training / # enrolled | Training attendance logs | Monthly | 0 (new program) | 20/year |
| Reduce household food insecurity | USDA 6-item Food Security Module score | Pre/post household survey | Baseline + 6 months + 12 months | 22% food insecure (zip code avg) | 15% food insecure among enrolled families |
| 10,000 lbs produce distributed | Lbs produced and distributed | Farm log + distribution records | Weekly | 4,800 lbs/year (2024) | 10,000 lbs by Month 12 |
| 75% of youth employed/in school at 6 months | % with positive outcome at 6-month follow-up | Follow-up survey | 6 months post-completion | N/A | 75% (15/20) |
| Community partnerships active | # referrals from partner organizations | Partner referral tracking | Monthly | 3 active MOUs | 6 active MOUs; 150 referrals/year |

**Data Analysis:**
- CSA food security: USDA 6-item Food Security Module administered at enrollment and at 6 and 12 months. Pre/post comparison. Results disaggregated by household size, income tier, zip code, and time enrolled. Comparison baseline: Clark County food insecurity rate (18%) as informal control.
- Youth outcomes: 6-month follow-up survey for all graduates. Employment status, wage rate, education enrollment, or apprenticeship participation recorded. Disaggregated by age, gender, school partner source.
- Produce efficiency: Weekly farm log entries. Monthly totals reviewed against crop plan. Variance over/under 10% triggers crop plan adjustment.

**Learning & Adaptation:**
Quarterly program review meetings (January, April, July, October) with full team. Agenda: indicator review, variance analysis, one program adjustment decision per meeting. Board receives quarterly dashboard. If CSA enrollment falls below 400 by Month 6, trigger: emergency outreach sprint with Three Square and school partners + CSA pricing review. Findings shared with USDA via required reporting and with Nevada Community Foundation via annual report.

**Reporting to Funder:**
- Interim report: Month 6 (narrative + data tables)
- Final report: Month 12 (full outcomes narrative + financial accounting + lessons learned)
- Responsible party: Executive Director with data compiled by Social Worker
- Format: USDA NIFA eforms portal submission

*Farm Manager — 0.5 FTE × $48,000/year = $24,000*
The Farm Manager directs all agricultural operations across all three farm sites, develops the crop plan, manages site staff, and tracks produce output data. 50% FTE allocation reflects the expansion from 2 to 3 farm sites funded by this grant.

*Youth Program Coordinator (new hire) — 0.35 FTE × $42,000/year = $7,200*
New position created specifically for this grant. The Youth Program Coordinator recruits and manages youth training cohorts, maintains training attendance records, and conducts 6-month follow-up surveys. Rate based on comparable youth services coordinator salaries in Clark County (Nevada Wage Survey 2024, $38,000-$46,000 range).

Fringe rate: 16% of personnel. Covers FICA (7.65%), workers' compensation (3.5%), and Nevada unemployment insurance (4.85%). Rate applied only to W-2 employees per organization fringe calculation. Calculation: $49,200 × 16% = $7,872.

### COST-EFFECTIVENESS STATEMENT

At full program operation, the total cost per household served is $267/year ($133,832 ÷ 500 households), or $5.14/week — delivering fresh produce access for less than the cost of a fast food meal. For youth workforce outcomes, the cost per trained participant is $6,692 ($133,832 ÷ 20 youth), compared to a national average of $9,800 for comparable workforce development programs (Urban Institute, 2023 Workforce Program Cost Analysis). Every dollar invested in this program produces an estimated $3.20 in combined social value: reduced emergency food costs, reduced healthcare costs associated with poor nutrition, and increased youth earning power.
